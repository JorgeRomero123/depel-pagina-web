# DEPEL - Página Web

## Design Context

### Users
Plant managers, procurement directors, and operations leads at pharmaceutical and industrial companies in Morelos and central México. They visit this site to vet DEPEL before awarding a contract. They're evaluating competence, safety track record, and whether DEPEL can handle the scale. They're busy, skeptical, and comparing against 2-3 other bids. The site must answer "can I trust these people with my facility?" in under 10 seconds.

### Brand Personality
**Bold, Expert, Driven.** DEPEL is not a timid contractor waiting for the phone to ring — they're an engineering firm that wins contracts by showing up prepared, knowing the NOM standards cold, and delivering on time. The voice is direct, confident, technically precise, but never arrogant. Think: the foreman who explains the plan clearly and then executes it perfectly.

### Emotional Target
**Confidence & Safety.** The site must make visitors feel: "These people won't let my plant down." Every design choice should reinforce reliability, technical competence, and zero-risk professionalism. This is not about delight or playfulness — it's about trust.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Mexican construction firm — real work, real crew, real trust.** Photo-heavy, showing the actual team in PPE at actual job sites. Less corporate polish, more authentic grit. The design should feel like a firm that gets their hands dirty and has the photos to prove it. NOT a glossy SaaS landing page. NOT a generic template with stock photos of people in suits shaking hands. The imagery should smell like a job site, not a boardroom.

- **Theme:** Light mode only. Clean white backgrounds throughout. Professional and straightforward.
- **Anti-references:** Generic AI websites with gradient blobs and abstract illustrations. SaaS-style glass morphism. Any design that could belong to a tech startup. Stock photos of "diverse team pointing at whiteboard."
- **Pro-references:** Mexican construction/engineering firms that lead with project photography. Caterpillar's commitment to showing real machinery and real scale. Schneider Electric's confidence in letting technical content breathe.

### Design Principles

1. **Show the work, not the words.** Photography of real DEPEL projects, real crew, real equipment should dominate over decorative illustrations or abstract graphics. If a section can be replaced by a strong photo, replace it. If you don't have a real photo, use a realistic industrial placeholder — never an illustration or icon-grid as the main visual.

2. **Earn trust in 10 seconds.** The hero and above-the-fold content must communicate: what DEPEL does, where they operate, and why they're safe to hire. Certifications (DC3, NOM) and concrete experience markers (years, project count) should be visible without scrolling.

3. **Confidence through restraint.** Don't over-animate, don't over-decorate, don't use too many colors at once. A confident brand doesn't need to try hard. Generous whitespace, strong typography, and deliberate color use (not gradients everywhere). Every hover effect and animation must feel purposeful, not performative.

4. **Build for the buyer, not the designer.** The target user is a 45-year-old plant manager on a laptop, not a design-awards jury. Prioritize scannability, clear hierarchy, and fast load times over visual cleverness. Every section should answer a specific buyer question: "What do you do?" "Can you handle my project?" "Who else have you worked for?" "How do I contact you?"

5. **Authenticity over polish.** Imperfect real photos beat perfect stock photos. Straightforward Spanish copy beats marketing buzzwords. A visible phone number beats a chatbot. The site should feel like it was built by people who build electrical systems — precise, functional, no wasted wire.
